<p>If you were to ask many physicists, I suspect that most would answer this question by talking about the beauty of the universe or mathematics, the thrill of discovery, or the fundamental role that physics plays in life. In other words, they would answer with the reasons why they chose to study the topic, not the reasons why one might want to teach it.</p>

<p>I think this makes sense. Professional physicists have obviously given a lot of thought to the topic that they have devoted much of their lives to, and most can speak reflectively about that choice. Although most of us are asked to teach the topic we are (presumably) experts at, the study of teaching itself is something that we engage with as something necessary to meet those obligations. In other words, we’re mostly passionate amateurs.</p> 

<p>I think the related question “why is physics taught?” might provide some insight. Two answers to it come to mind. The first is that physics is taught to provide students who require the subject for their careers with an introduction to the fundamentals prior to their study of specialized and advanced applications in their major courses. This answer clearly applies to those in engineering or other physical sciences, but perhaps only applies to a subset of those in the life sciences. The second answer comes from physicists themselves. We often describe what we’re teaching not as physics itself, but as problem solving and creativity.</p> 

<p>How we teach physics matters too. There’s been a lot of interest in practices, and much of that has shown that chalkboard lectures and theoretical derivations just don’t teach beginners very much. From a student’s perspective, rote memorization of formulas is both a strategy that is quickly acquired and one that leads to little in the way of problem solving or creativity. We need to do better. </p>
  
<p>Perhaps we can find some insights from the practice of physics.</p> 

<p>One way to view a scientific paper is through narratives. Manuscripts tell us stories of how researchers considered different approaches and in what ways those attempts at problem solving succeeded or failed. These narratives are different from a creative piece in what they reference - data, equations, statistics, and the like. Rather that evaluating the pieces before us in terms of aesthetics, we think about them in terms of these tools and standards.</p> 

<p>Physicists, perhaps in their own self interest, sometimes mistake these things for an abstract notion of creativity or some generalized ability to solve problems. In the classroom, it is hoped that a student of physics will have practiced this process and be able to apply a new perspective to their own lives. In this way, not only do we produce more and better physicists, but we also hope to produce more versatile doctors, engineers and playwrights.</p>

<p>That is to say, we teach physics because physics is unique. It’s not unique in its reliance on mathematics, or even the subject of inquiry. Indeed, there is a great deal of overlap between physics and mathematics when it comes to theory, and even more overlap between the work of condensed matter experimentalists and materials engineers.</p> 

<p>Instead, what is unique about physics is what is found in the standards of evidence and the ways of reasoning that physicists insist on. Physics isn't a body of knowledge but a way of looking for answers in nature. It is a human endeavor. Put another way, what is unique about physics is not found in physics, but in whatever it is that is unique about physicists. </p>
