<p>Cause and effect might be such a fundamental fact about the world that one wonders why it bears mentioning. After all, a gazelle might observe her compatriots running after being startled, and correctly infer the presence of a rather large feline as the cause of the commotion. This seems like both a valid and valuable inference!</p>

<p>What’s not surprising then is that notions like causality exist in modern relativistic theories. And in those theories, causality becomes tied to the idea of spacetime. And since our best guess is that we live in a FLRW spacetime, we might want to ask questions about whether our causal notions are consistent with our spacetime ones. What is an FLRW spacetime then? Named for several pioneering cosmologists who first considered it, it’s one distinguished by having a metric that looks like (in “natural units”): </p>

ds<sup>2</sup>  = dt<sup>2</sup> + a(t)<sup>2</sup>d&Sigma;<sup>2</sup>

<p>Why is this our best guess for a description of the real world? The short answer if you look at the d&Sigma; term is that it’s homogeneous (the same everywhere) and isotropic (the same in every direction), which are properties we might expect the universe to have <em> a priori </em>. </p>

<p>The exact form here is however a bit unusual. We have a term a(t) that scales a spatial term and is (naturally) called the scale factor. As the scale factor changes, the universe gets bigger everywhere. This is why there is no “center” to the expansion of the universe. And indeed, a variety of lines of evidence tell us that a(t) was smaller in the past and will get bigger in the future - this is the “classical” big bang theory.</p>

<p>There’s a curious question for causality if we think about the scale factor changing for the universe as a whole - namely how are changes in the scale factor communicated across space? Put the way a colleague recently posed the question, how do places infinitely separated (e.g. possibly but not necessarily in an infinite universe) know that the scale factor has changed and respond accordingly?</p>

<p>If this doesn’t seem like an obvious question to ask, consider the related question that science enthusiasts often ask - if galaxies farther away from us are moving faster, then do some of them eventually move faster than the speed of light? It turns out that both the answer is yes and that this question is almost the same as the one we’ve just asked.</p> 

<p>The simple answer to both is that changes to the scale factor aren’t communicated at all. You can show from the Einstein equations that a(t) is a consequence of things that are going on locally, so the fact that a(t) has a different value at different times is not communicated spatially, but is always determined by what is happening locally. That a(t) has one value for the “universe” as a whole is a consequence of the assumptions used to derive the FLRW metric. How justified we are in talking about such global properties is an open question.</p> 

<p>Were we to live in a universe with spatially evolving changes to the metric (e.g. writing something like a(r,t), instead of a(t)) we would then need to make sure that those changes were communicated causally. Well, at least as far as we believe in causality at all, because what is genuinely puzzling (and surprising) here is that causality does not (seem) to emerge from more fundamental concerns. This, however, is a topic for another time.</p>
